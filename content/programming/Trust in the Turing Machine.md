## How is _Human_ related to _Mathematics_

If _mathematics_ `m`, as a science, is a chain or tree of logical reasoning that can be contained in _Mathematician_ `M` (in his mind) as an actor of such science, then it can be said that it is the work of some _Mathematician_ `M` in `X`.
`m = W(M, X)`
Where `X` is _Truth_.

The reverse is also true. If _Mathematician_ `M` is an entity capable of being present in _mathematics_ `m` as a science, then it can be said that _Mathematician_ is a projection of _mathematics_ `m` onto some truth `X`.
`M = P(m, X)`;

That is, `X` is an object or entity that, possessing the fullness, reflecting in the creativity of a participant in some work process, can then recreate the participant itself, representing an all-encompassing surface onto which the projection of the results of its work falls. The DNA principle.

Let’s assume that some _conclusion_ `d`, reached by _Human_ `H`, is the result of the work of _Human_ in some `X`.
`d = W(H, X)`
and vice versa
`H = P(d, X)`

Let’s also say that _work_ `W` is the creation of something not just new, but fundamentally new or better than what exists:
`W(A, D) = B`
where `B` is a significant step from `A`.
Let’s also say that work cannot go beyond the system within which it is produced
`W(D, D) ≠ E`
`W(D, D) = D`
Thus, repetition is indeed, if not the mother of learning, at least a bell to open the textbook.

And projection is the unfolding of what served as the primary source. It can be said, the recreation of the creator from the results of his labor.
`P(B, D) = A`

> Of course, if the creator did not work in truth `X`, but dealt only with distorting concepts like `D`, then such a creator is hardly subject to "resurrection" in the form in which everyone knew him. The other thing is that he was mainly known probably only by liars.
#### Let’s assume that _Mathematician_ is a _Human_:
`M = H`
then `P(M, H) = H = M`:
`W(M, X) = W(H, X)`
therefore:
`m' = m`

This means that the result of human labor `m'` does not expand the concept of `m`. One can conclude that engaging in mathematics is a completely useless activity. One could end here, but...
##### Let’s assume the opposite (_Mathematician_ is not a _Human_):
`M ≠ H`
then, if a person takes a couple of lessons:
`P(M, H) = M'`
and works a bit:
`W(M', X) ≠ W(M, X)` -> `m' ≠ m`
and thus:
`m' ∪ m = m'` (in case `M ⊅ H`)
therefore:
`m' = W(P(M, H), X)`
the reverse is also true:
`P(m', X) = M' = P(M, H)`
### Conclusion
A _Human_ solving a new mathematical problem includes himself in the set of _Mathematicians_, thereby expanding both concepts (_"Mathematics"_ and _"Mathematician"_).

It is also evident that _Mathematician_ is not a _Human_, at least not in the full sense of the concept of _Human_ and not its superset. It is also not a fact that a Human can represent a superset of a Mathematician.

## _Turing Machine_ in _Mathematics_

Let _Turing Machine_ `Mt` be a projection of some _machine mathematics_ `mt` onto _Mathematician_ `M` (of course, onto his way of thinking). That is, a certain _Machine_ capable of independently solving tasks that _Mathematician_ M has, but receiving requests and providing answers within some _machine mathematics_ `mt`. Or in other words, a kind of ergonomic individual chair on which a mathematician can comfortably sit while solving a specific problem:
`Mt = P(mt, M)`

Let the reverse also be true, that _machine mathematics_ is the result of the work of _Mathematician_ on the _Turing Machine_. In the context of programming, this process can be compared to decompiling code, where the code is `Mt`, the sought language is `mt`, and the reverse engineer is `M`:
`mt = W(M, Mt)`

(It is obvious that the _Turing Machine_ cannot be greater than the _Mathematician_, and it is at least not interesting for the _Mathematician_ to work in the interests of just making `Mt` better for better.)

Then the increase in _machine mathematics_ `dt` is the work of some projection of _Human_ in _Turing Space_ - `Ht = P(H, Mt)` on the Mathematician:
`dt' = W(Ht, M) = W(P(H, Mt), M)`,

where _Human_ in _Turing Space_ is one who programs the _Turing Machine_ to solve tasks for the _Mathematician_.

Consequently, similarly to the previous example:
`mt' = W(Ht, M) + W(Mt, M)`

That is, by bringing his _Turing Machine_ into widespread use, the _Mathematician_ provides himself with sufficient information to derive a new _machine mathematics_ and, as a consequence, the possibility of building a new generation of machine:
`Mt' = P(mt', M)`

### Conclusion
The increase in _machine mathematics_ represents the work of the operator of the Turing Machine for the real _Mathematician_. This, as a consequence, opens up possibilities for creating new _Machines_.

## _Human_ in _Turing Space_

### _Mathematician_ and _Machine_
It is evident that the _Turing Machine_ `Mt` by itself, even in conjunction with the _Mathematician_, cannot advance mathematics in any way:
`W(M, X) ∪ W(Mt, X) = W(M, X) ∪ W(P(mt, M), X) = W(M, X) = m`

Yes, it is true, `W(Mt, X)` can calculate a lot for us, even some of the largest prime numbers, but we still will not go beyond the boundaries of mathematics `m`, i.e., we will not produce anything fundamentally new. We may prove some theorems that seemed plausible even without proof, but no more.

That is, without introducing _Human_ in any form into the equation, progress is impossible. Accordingly, the following makes sense:
`m" = W(P(M, H), X/mt)`
That is, for the unnecessary calculation of large numbers, the work of the _Mathematician_ is reduced to working with a simplified form of truth at an abstraction level that excludes the use of ultra-precise calculations outside the _Turing Machine_. One could say that part of the work is simply delegated to the _Machine_ in order to engage in more interesting tasks.

Then, by performing the following transformation, we derive:
`P(m", X) = M"`

Where `M"` is still a _Mathematician_, but no longer existing separately from the _Turing Machine_ `Mt`, because he needs it to simplify part of his work.

#### Evolution of Machines
Accordingly, it makes sense to write
`iMt = P(imt, M")`
Where `imt` is some _intelligent Turing mathematics_, `M"` is the _Mathematician_ who is already familiar with the previous generation of _machines_ and has formed a new list of tasks based on the solutions already obtained through the previous iteration. And `iMt` is the _intelligent Turing machine_, the requirements of which arise from the tasks facing the mathematician and lie in the space of `imt`.

If we recall the reasons for the emergence of `mt` - _machine mathematics_, such reasons were the work on the first prototypes of computing devices, such as Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine. The study of such machines and the creation of descriptive forms leads us to the emergence of the first programming languages and specifications, based on which subsequent generations of such devices were built. Of course, Babbage faced colossal limitations, and undoubtedly the `mt` he used had to borrow partly from the real world, probably from the machine engineering industry.

Today, rules and laws from the field of `mt` can be used to build the first `imt`, which we are already observing in the field of artificial intelligence. We already have `iMt` solutions that participate in science.

`im = W(iM, X)`

where `im` is _intelligent mathematics_, which cannot exist without the application of iMt solutions. And `iM` is the _intelligent mathematician_ working on the subsequent list of problems in mathematics. He is likely creating the next generation of _Turing Machines_ `xMt` that solve relevant tasks for him, operating on the basis of some _secret machine mathematics_ `xmt`:
`xMt = P(xmt, iM)`

Then, by analogy with the method of creating the _Turing Machine_, let’s create a new human mathematics:
`W(iM, X) = P(S(m..m"), H) = im`

`P(W(iMt, M"), H) = im`
But we remember that:
`W(iMt, M") <= M"`
This means that if `H` does not have some extra free `im` hidden in his pocket, then the equation is not valid.
`P(M", H) ≠ im`

This only means that we are not really interested in the results of the work of `iMt`, because they point to possibly non-existent but not new solutions. But how can we use the results of its labor?

Let’s take the best of possible solutions, intentionally discard the results of the work of `iMt`, just as we did with all other machines:
`im = W(P(M", H), X/imt)`

That is, as a _Mathematician_, we will try to work with `X` in such a way that `imt = S(m..m")` does not enter the space of our reasoning. Consequently, we will have a much greater chance of creating an original solution.
